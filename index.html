Building in Public: How I'm Teaching Myself to Think Like a Quant

October 21, 2025

I don't think becoming "good at quant" is about memorizing libraries, grinding LeetCode forever, or stacking flashy projects on GitHub. For me, it's been about something quieter (and harder): learning how to think.

This is a short snapshot of how I'm approaching that process right now ‚Äî not as an expert, but as someone actively figuring it out.

Thinking > Tools

Early on, I thought progress meant learning more tools. New Python packages. New frameworks. New buzzwords. And sure, tools matter ‚Äî but I've learned they're only useful once the thinking is already there.

Math has been the biggest driver of that shift. Classes like probability, linear algebra, and analysis don't just teach techniques; they force precision. You learn what assumptions you're making, what breaks when they're wrong, and how small changes ripple through an argument. That mindset transfers directly to data, models, and markets.

Coding as a Thinking Tool

I don't code just to "build things." I code to clarify ideas.

If I can't simulate it, I probably don't understand it. If I can't explain it in code, my mental model is probably fuzzy. Writing small scripts ‚Äî especially for probability or data problems ‚Äî forces me to be honest about what I actually know versus what I'm hand-waving.

Why I Build in Public

Putting work on GitHub (even when it's imperfect) keeps me accountable. It forces me to finish thoughts, clean up reasoning, and document why I did something ‚Äî not just what I did.

Building in public isn't about showing off progress ‚Äî it's about making learning visible.

How I Decide What's Worth Learning (And What I Ignore)

November 14, 2025

One of the hardest parts of learning anything technical isn't understanding the material ‚Äî it's deciding what's worth your time in the first place.

Does This Strengthen My Thinking or Just My Toolkit?

The first question I ask is whether something strengthens how I think, or whether it just adds another tool to the pile.

Things that consistently make the cut: Math that forces precision (probability, linear algebra, proofs), concepts that transfer across problems, and ideas that change how I frame questions.

Can I Explain This Simply?

If I can't explain something in plain language ‚Äî or better yet, implement a small version of it ‚Äî I probably don't understand it well enough to justify going deeper.

Does This Compound?

Some things compound quietly. Others don't. Learning a core mathematical idea tends to pay dividends across everything else. Learning a niche library often doesn't.

Why I'm More Interested in Models Than Answers

December 21, 2025

For a long time, I cared a lot about answers. Getting the right result. Matching the solution. Knowing I was "correct."

Somewhere along the way ‚Äî mostly through math and probability ‚Äî that shifted. Now, I'm far more interested in models than answers.

Answers Are Fragile

An answer is usually tied to a very specific setup. Change an assumption, tweak a parameter, or move slightly outside the original context, and the answer can fall apart.

Models Force You to Be Honest

A model, even a simple one, forces clarity. What assumptions are you making? What are you ignoring? What happens when those assumptions fail?

Models Age Better Than Answers

Answers expire quickly. Models don't. A numerical result might only be useful today. A good model ‚Äî even an imperfect one ‚Äî can be adapted, stress-tested, and reused as conditions change.
