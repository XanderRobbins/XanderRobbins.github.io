Two Representations

Every nonzero complex number z has two equivalent forms:

z = x + iy = r · eiθ = r(cos θ + i sin θ)

where r = |z| = √(x²+y²) is the modulus and θ = Arg(z) ∈ (−π, π] is the principal argument.

Euler's Formula

eiθ = cos θ + i sin θ

This is provable from the Taylor series of ez, cos z, sin z. The key consequence: eiθ lives on the unit circle for all real θ. Try dragging the point near the unit circle (|z|≈1).

The Complex Exponential

ez = ex+iy = ex(cos y + i sin y)
ez ≠ 0 for any z ∈ ℂ. This follows from |ez| = ex > 0 always.
⚠ Arg(z) is discontinuous on ℂ\{0}. It jumps across the negative real axis (from +π to −π). This forces us to make a "branch cut" for Log(z).

Multiple Arguments

arg(z) = Arg(z) + 2πk, k ∈ ℤ

Any argument differs from the principal one by a multiple of 2π. So z = reiθ = rei(θ+2πk) for all k ∈ ℤ.

Conjugate in Polar Form

z̄ = re−iθ z·z̄ = r² = |z|²

The Polar Multiplication Rule

For z₁ = r₁eiθ₁ and z₂ = r₂eiθ₂:

z₁ · z₂ = r₁r₂ · ei(θ₁+θ₂)

Multiply the moduli and add the arguments. Geometrically: multiplying by z₂ scales by r₂ and rotates by θ₂.

De Moivre's Theorem

zn = rn · einθ = rn(cos nθ + i sin nθ)

Division & Inverse

z₁/z₂ = (r₁/r₂)·ei(θ₁−θ₂) 1/z = z̄/|z|² = r⁻¹e−iθ
⚠ Warning on Arg: Arg(z₁z₂) ≠ Arg(z₁)+Arg(z₂) in general! The sum may lie outside (−π,π]. Only with ±2πk does additivity hold.

Rectangular Formula

z₁z₂ = (x₁x₂−y₁y₂) + i(x₁y₂+y₁x₂)

Notice this encodes the matrix multiplication for 2×2 matrices of the form [[a,−b],[b,a]]. Every complex number corresponds to such a rotation-scaling matrix!

Proposition: If z₁z₂ = 0 then z₁=0 or z₂=0 (ℂ has no zero divisors).

Cauchy-Riemann Equations

Sections 17–19 · The necessary (and almost sufficient) condition for complex differentiability

The Setup

Write f(z) = u(x,y) + iv(x,y). If f is complex-differentiable at z₀=(x₀,y₀), evaluating the limit along the real axis and then along the imaginary axis must give the same answer. This forces:

u_x = v_y      v_x = −u_y

These are the Cauchy-Riemann (CR) equations. When they hold and partials are continuous: f′(z) = u_x + iv_x = v_y − iu_y

Necessity vs. Sufficiency

Theorem (Necessary): If f is complex-differentiable at z₀, then the CR equations hold at z₀.
Theorem (Sufficient): If CR holds at z₀ and the partial derivatives are continuous at z₀, then f is differentiable at z₀ and f′(z₀) = u_x + iv_x.
⚠ CR alone is NOT sufficient! A function can satisfy CR at a point but be non-differentiable there. The continuity of partials is essential. (See the counterexample in your notes: the piecewise function that satisfies CR at 0 but isn't differentiable there.)

CR in Polar Form

Writing f = u(r,θ) + iv(r,θ), the CR equations become:

r · u_r = v_θ     r · v_r = −u_θ

When satisfied with continuous partials:

f′(z) = e−iθ(u_r + iv_r)

Polar Example: ez

eiθ = cos θ + i sin θ means u=cos θ, v=sin θ. Then u_r=0, v_θ=−sin θ... wait, let's use z=reiθ for ez in rectangular form instead. The polar form is most useful for Log, √z, etc.

Matrix Interpretation

If f is complex-differentiable, the Jacobian of f viewed as ℝ²→ℝ² must be of the form:

J_f = [[u_x, u_y], [v_x, v_y]] = [[a, −b], [b, a]]

i.e., a rotation-scaling matrix. This is exactly what CR says: u_x=v_y (=a) and v_x=−u_y (=b). So complex differentiability = the real Jacobian is a conformal (angle-preserving) linear map.

Analytic Functions

Sections 20–22 · The central objects of complex analysis

Definitions

Analytic on G: f is complex-differentiable at every point of the open set G.

Analytic at z₀: f is analytic on some N_ε(z₀).

Entire: f is analytic on all of ℂ.

Entire Functions

Polynomials, ez, sin z, cos z. Their sums, products, and compositions.

Huge contrast with ℝ: Over ℝ, once-differentiable doesn't even imply twice-differentiable. But in ℂ: analytic ⟹ infinitely differentiable ⟹ equals its Taylor series. This is a theorem!

Non-Analytic Functions

• f(z) = z̄: CR gives u_x=1, v_y=−1 — fails everywhere. nowhere analytic

• f(z) = |z|²: CR holds only at z=0. not analytic anywhere

• f(z) = Re(z): CR gives 1=0. never differentiable

• f(z) = |z|: CR only at z=0, partials not continuous. nowhere analytic

Key Identities

cos z = (eiz+e−iz)/2 sin z = (eiz−e−iz)/(2i)

Both entire. Note: |sin z| and |cos z| are unbounded on ℂ (unlike on ℝ)!

Key Theorems

f′≡0 ⟹ f constant (on a connected domain G)
If f′=0 everywhere on G, then u_x=v_x=0 everywhere. If two points are connected by a line segment parameterized as γ(s), then d/ds[f(γ(s))] = f′(γ(s))·γ′(s) = 0, so f is constant on the segment. Connectedness of G extends this to all of G.
Re(f) constant ⟹ f constant (analytic on connected domain)
If u(x,y) = c (constant), then u_x = u_y = 0. By CR: v_x = −u_y = 0 and v_y = u_x = 0. So f′ = u_x + iv_x = 0. By the previous theorem, f is constant.
|f| constant ⟹ f constant (analytic on connected domain)
f analytic, Im(f)=0 ⟹ f constant (connected domain)

Isolated Zeros & Identity Theorem

Isolated Zeros Lemma

If f is analytic, f≢0 near z₀, but f(z₀)=0, then ∃m≥1 (the order of the zero) and analytic g with g(z₀)≠0 such that:

f(z) = (z−z₀)m · g(z)

The zeros of a nonzero analytic function are isolated.

Identity Theorem: If f, g analytic on domain G, and f(z_n)=g(z_n) for a sequence {z_n}→z₀ ∈ G, then f≡g on G.
Power of this: An analytic function is completely determined by its values on any convergent sequence in its domain. Two analytic functions that agree on a tiny arc are equal everywhere on the domain!
Analytic Continuation: The analytic extension of f to a larger domain is unique if it exists.

Harmonic Functions

Section 21 · Blue = u-level curves, Green = v-level curves (they're perpendicular!)

Level curves u=c cross v=c at right angles — a visual proof that analytic functions are conformal (angle-preserving).

Definition

A real-valued function h: G→ℝ is harmonic if it has continuous second-order partial derivatives and satisfies the Laplace equation:

Δh = h_xx + h_yy = 0

Analytic ⟹ Harmonic Parts

Theorem: If f = u+iv is analytic on G, then both u and v are harmonic on G.
From CR: u_x = v_y and u_y = −v_x. Differentiate the first: u_xx = v_yx. Differentiate the second: −v_xy = u_yy. Since partials are continuous (a consequence of analyticity), v_yx = v_xy. So u_xx = v_yx = v_xy = −u_yy, giving u_xx + u_yy = 0. ✓ Same argument works for v.

Harmonic Conjugates

Given harmonic u on G, a harmonic conjugate is v such that f = u+iv is analytic.

Uniqueness: Any two harmonic conjugates of u on domain G differ by a real constant.
Existence: If G is simply connected and u is harmonic on G, then u has a harmonic conjugate.
Counterexample to existence on non-simply connected domain: u = ln|z| is harmonic on ℂ\{0}. Its harmonic conjugate would be Arg(z), but Arg is discontinuous on ℂ\{0}. The problem is ℂ\{0} is not simply connected.

Practical Trick

If f = u+iv is analytic on G, then u is automatically harmonic — no computation needed! Use this to save time:

u = Re(f) is harmonic whenever f is analytic

For example: Re(z²) = x²−y² is harmonic; Re(ez) = excos y is harmonic; Re(Log z) = ln|z| = ln√(x²+y²) is harmonic on ℂ\{0}.

The Problem with log

We want log as an inverse of ez. But ez+2πi=ez, so ez is not injective — many z map to the same w. Thus log is multi-valued:

log(z) = ln|z| + i·arg(z) = ln|z| + i(Arg(z)+2πk)

Each choice of k ∈ ℤ gives a different branch of the logarithm.

Principal Branch Log

Defined on G = ℂ\(−∞, 0] (cut along negative real axis):

Log(z) = ln|z| + i·Arg(z), Arg(z) ∈ (−π, π)

Properties:

• eLog(z) = z for all z ∈ G

• Log is analytic on G, and (Log z)′ = 1/z

⚠ Log(z₁z₂) ≠ Log z₁ + Log z₂ in general! This equality holds only when Arg z₁ + Arg z₂ ∈ (−π,π). If the sum of arguments "wraps around" past ±π, you get a ±2πi discrepancy.

General Branches

A function f: G→ℂ is a branch of log on G if ef(z)=z for all z∈G.

Theorem: Two branches on the same connected domain differ by 2πin for a fixed n∈ℤ.
Theorem: Any branch of log is analytic and has derivative 1/z.

Branch Cuts

A branch cut is a curve removed from ℂ to make arg single-valued and continuous. It prevents "circling the origin." Branch cuts need not be straight lines!

Why the cut is necessary: If we try to extend Log to all of ℂ\{0}, we'd need Arg to be continuous there. But going around the origin, Arg increases by 2π — a jump must occur somewhere. The cut is where we accept that jump.

Power Function zc

Section 25 · Branches of zc for complex c

Definition via Log

For c ∈ ℂ and a branch f of log on domain G:

zc := ec·f(z)

defines a branch of the power function on G. Different branches of log give different branches of zc.

Principal Branch

zc = ec · Log(z), z ∉ (−∞, 0]

Example: √z

Take c = 1/2 and use Log(z) = ln|z| + iArg(z):

√z = e½ Log z = e½(ln|z|+iArg z) = √|z| · eiArg(z)/2

This is the principal square root. It extends the positive real square root to G = ℂ\(−∞,0].

The other branch (using Log z + 2πi) gives:

e½(Log z + 2πi) = e½Log z·eπi = −√z

So the two branches of √z are just negatives of each other — as expected!

Example: ii

Here z=i, c=i. We have |i|=1, Arg(i)=π/2. So:

ii = ei·Log(i) = ei(ln1 + iπ/2) = ei·iπ/2 = e−π/2 ≈ 0.208

ii is real! (Using the principal branch.) Other branches give e−π/2+2πk for k∈ℤ.

General Behavior

Using branch f(z) = Log z + 2πin:

zc = ec(Log z + 2πin) = ecLog z·e2πinc

Different branches relate by multiplying by e2πinc. If c is a rational p/q (in lowest terms), there are exactly q distinct branches.

Important Special Cases

c ∈ ℤ (integer power)

e2πinc=1 for all n — all branches give the same value! So zn for integer n is single-valued, consistent with z·z·…·z.

c = 1/n (nth root)

e2πin·(1/n) = e2πi = 1... but e2πik/n ≠ 1 for k=1,…,n-1. So there are exactly n branches of z1/n — the nth roots.

Derivative: Any branch of zc has derivative c·zc−1 (using the same branch).
⚠ zc with complex c is NOT the same as repeated multiplication — it's defined via the logarithm and is branch-dependent!

Definition

Given a contour C parameterized by w: [a,b]→ℂ (continuous, piecewise C¹) and f continuous on C:

∫_C f(z) dz := ∫_a^b f(w(t)) · w′(t) dt

This reduces to a standard real integral of a complex-valued function of t.

ML Inequality

Theorem: If |f(z)| ≤ M on C and L(C) = arc length of C, then |∫_C f dz| ≤ M·L
|∫_C f(z) dz| ≤ M · L(C)
Main use: Proving that integrals along large arcs (R→∞) go to zero. If M→0 faster than L(C)=2πR→∞, the integral vanishes.

The Fundamental Calculation

∮|z|=1 zn dz = 2πi if n = −1 0 if n ≠ −1 (n ∈ ℤ)

Proof sketch: Parameterize by w(t)=eit, t∈[0,2π]. Then w′(t)=ieit and zn = eint, giving ∫₀²π eint·ieitdt = i∫₀²π ei(n+1)tdt.

For n≠−1: ∫₀²π ei(n+1)tdt = [ei(n+1)t/(i(n+1))]₀²π = 0.

For n=−1: ∫₀²π dt = 2π, times i gives 2πi. ✓

This 2πi is the heart of everything! Cauchy's integral formula, residues, everything flows from this.

Properties

Linearity: ∫_C (αf+βg) = α∫f + β∫g

Reversal: ∫_{−C} f = −∫_C f

Concatenation: ∫_{C₁+C₂} f = ∫_{C₁} f + ∫_{C₂} f

Reparameterization: The value doesn't depend on choice of parameterization (as long as orientation is preserved).

Cauchy-Goursat Theorem

Theorem: If f is analytic on a simply connected domain G, and C ⊂ G is any closed contour, then ∮_C f(z) dz = 0.
Equivalently: A closed-loop integral of an analytic function is zero. No singularities inside = zero integral.

Simply Connected Domains

A domain G is simply connected if it is connected and every closed curve in G can be continuously shrunk to a point while staying in G. Informally: no holes.

• ℂ itself: simply connected ✓

• Any disk {|z|<R}: simply connected ✓

• ℂ\{0}: NOT simply connected (the origin is a hole) ✗

• {0<|z|<1} (punctured disk): NOT simply connected ✗

• A domain with a hole (like an annulus): NOT simply connected ✗

Corollaries

Antiderivative existence: If G is simply connected and f is analytic on G, then f has an analytic antiderivative F on G (F′=f).
Path independence: On a simply connected domain, ∫_C f depends only on the endpoints of C, not the path.
Harmonic conjugates: On a simply connected domain, every harmonic u has a harmonic conjugate v.
Counterexample (not simply connected): ∮_{|z|=1} (1/z) dz = 2πi ≠ 0. Here f=1/z has a singularity at z=0 inside the contour, and the domain ℂ\{0} is not simply connected.

Antiderivatives & Path Independence

Section 31 · The TFAE theorem and using antiderivatives

The Big Theorem (TFAE)

If f is continuous on a domain G, the following are equivalent:

1. f has an antiderivative F on G (F analytic, F′ = f) 2. Path independence: for any two contours C₁, C₂ in G with the same endpoints, ∫_{C₁} f = ∫_{C₂} f 3. For every closed contour C in G: ∮_C f(z) dz = 0

Furthermore, if F is an antiderivative and C goes from z₁ to z₂:

∫_C f(z) dz = F(z₂) − F(z₁)

Using This in Practice

To evaluate ∫_C f(z)dz when f has an antiderivative F:

∫_C f(z) dz = F(endpoint) − F(start)

This is exactly the complex FTC!

Example

Evaluate ∫_C z² dz from z=0 to z=1+i (any path).

F(z) = z³/3 is an antiderivative of z² ∫_C z² dz = F(1+i) − F(0) = (1+i)³/3 − 0 = (1+i)²(1+i)/3 = (2i)(1+i)/3 = (−2+2i)/3

Since z² is entire (analytic on all ℂ, simply connected), the integral is path-independent.

When Antiderivatives Fail

f = 1/z does NOT have an antiderivative on ℂ\{0} because:

∮_{|z|=1} (1/z) dz = 2πi ≠ 0

If it had one, the integral around any closed loop would be 0 (by condition 3).

However, 1/z does have an antiderivative on any simply connected subdomain of ℂ\{0}: that antiderivative is a branch of Log!

Subtlety: ∮ z−1 dz can be zero on some closed curves (those not encircling z=0) but 2πi on others. This is why the domain matters — antiderivative existence is a global property of the domain.

Step-by-Step: Integrating on a Branch Cut

To evaluate ∫_C f(z)dz when the obvious antiderivative has a branch cut problem:

Step 1: Check if the contour crosses the branch cut of the natural antiderivative.

Step 2: If it does, choose a different branch of the logarithm (with a different cut) that the contour avoids.

Step 3: Verify the new branch is analytic on a domain containing the contour.

Step 4: Apply the FTC with the new branch.

Key insight: Different branches of Log give the same derivative (1/z) but different antiderivative values. Choose the branch based on which one is analytic along your path!

Simply Connected Equivalences

Your notes list 8 equivalent conditions for a domain G to be simply connected. The key ones for this exam:

• Every closed contour integral of an analytic f equals 0

• Every analytic f on G has an antiderivative

• Every harmonic function on G has a harmonic conjugate

• Every nonvanishing analytic f has an analytic square root

• Every nonvanishing analytic f has an analytic logarithm

These are all "equivalent" to the topological notion of simple connectedness — no holes!
