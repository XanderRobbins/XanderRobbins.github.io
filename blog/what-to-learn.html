How I Decide What's Worth Learning (And What I Ignore)

November 14, 2025

One of the hardest parts of learning anything technical isn't understanding the material — it's deciding what's worth your time in the first place.

There's no shortage of things you could learn. New tools, new fields, new buzzwords, new threads telling you that if you don't know X by tomorrow, you're behind. Early on, I tried to chase all of it. That didn't last long.

Over time, I've started using a few simple filters to decide what I actually focus on — and just as importantly, what I ignore.

Does This Strengthen My Thinking or Just My Toolkit?

The first question I ask is whether something strengthens how I think, or whether it just adds another tool to the pile.

Things that consistently make the cut: Math that forces precision (probability, linear algebra, proofs), concepts that transfer across problems, and ideas that change how I frame questions.

Things that usually don't: Tools I don't have a concrete use for yet, frameworks I'm learning "just in case," and anything I can only use by copying patterns.

Tools are easy to pick up later. Thinking is not.

Can I Explain This Simply?

If I can't explain something in plain language — or better yet, implement a small version of it — I probably don't understand it well enough to justify going deeper.

This is where coding helps. Writing a short script or notebook forces clarity. There's no hiding behind vague intuition once you have to make something run.

If an idea collapses the moment I try to formalize it, that's a signal to slow down — not move on.

Does This Compound?

Some things compound quietly. Others don't.

Learning a core mathematical idea tends to pay dividends across everything else. Learning a niche library often doesn't. That doesn't mean niche tools are bad — just that they're lower priority early on.

I try to bias toward things that make future learning easier, unlock multiple domains at once, and stay relevant even as tools change.

If something only helps in one narrow context, I'm comfortable skipping it for now.

What Am I Willing to Be Bad At (For Now)?

This might be the most important filter.

You can't be good at everything at once. Trying to be just spreads effort thin. So I've gotten more comfortable explicitly choosing gaps — areas I know I'm weak in, but am intentionally postponing.

That choice isn't permanent. It's just honest.

Being deliberate about what I ignore has been as valuable as deciding what to learn.

Avoiding the Distraction Loop

There's a certain kind of productivity that looks like progress but isn't. Watching videos. Skimming docs. Saving links. Feeling busy.

When I notice myself doing that, it's usually a sign I need a sharper question — not more information.

Learning accelerates once you're solving a real problem. Until then, restraint matters more than curiosity.

This Will Change — and That's Fine

None of this is fixed. As my interests evolve, so will these filters.

But for now, this framework keeps me grounded. It helps me say no without guilt, focus without panic, and learn without constantly feeling behind.

In a world with infinite things to learn, attention is the real scarce resource.
